  ---                                                                                                                                        
  What the model is trying to predict                                                                                                        
                                                                                                                                             
  Two targets:                                                                                                                               
  - mood_delta — how much did the participant's mood change after a session? (self-reported before vs after, scale shift)
  - stress_delta — how much did physiological stress change during the session?

  ---
  What it uses as input (features)

  The features fall into three groups:

  Circadian context — where is this person in their normal daily rhythm?
  - baseline_deviation_entry — is the participant more or less stressed than usual for this time of day? (raw difference from their
  non-session hourly average)
  - hr_baseline_deviation — same but for heart rate
  - hour_of_day, day_of_week

  Session context
  - playlist_calm / playlist_energy — which playlist type was played
  - mood_before_score — self-reported mood before the session
  - bb_start — Body Battery at start (energy reserve)
  - days_since_last_session — recovery time between sessions
  - pre_state_encoded — activity just before (sedentary / light / active)

  During/after biometrics
  - during_stress_mean, post_stress_mean, during_hr_mean, post_hr_mean

  Plus participant one-hot columns to capture individual differences.

  ---
  How it's validated: Leave-One-Out (LOO)

  For each session: train on all other sessions, predict this one. Repeat for every session. This simulates real-world use — can you predict
  an outcome for a session you haven't seen?

  This is stricter than a normal train/test split, which is appropriate here given the small N (~82 sessions).

  ---
  The models

  Four are compared:
  - DummyMean — always predicts the average. This is the baseline to beat. If your model can't beat this, you've learned nothing.
  - Ridge — linear regression with regularization. Interpretable via coefficients.
  - Random Forest — ensemble of decision trees. Captures non-linear patterns.
  - Gradient Boosting — sequential trees that correct prior errors. Powerful but prone to overfitting on small data.

  ---
  What the results mean

  For mood_delta (82 sessions):

  ┌──────────────────┬──────┬──────────┐
  │      Model       │ MAE  │ R² (LOO) │
  ├──────────────────┼──────┼──────────┤
  │ DummyMean        │ 1.82 │ -0.02    │
  ├──────────────────┼──────┼──────────┤

  - MAE = 1.65 means the model is off by ±1.65 mood points on average. Given the scale (mood changes ranging from -8 to +7), this is moderate
   — not great, not terrible.
  - R² = 0.25 means the model explains about 25% of the variance in mood change. The remaining 75% is noise or unmeasured factors.
  - GradientBoosting overfits badly (train R²=0.81, LOO R²=0.06) — it memorizes the training data but generalizes poorly.

  For stress_delta (65 sessions):

  ┌──────────────┬──────┬──────────┐

  ├──────────────┼──────┼──────────┤

  ├──────────────┼──────┼──────────┤

  └──────────────┴──────┴──────────┘

  - **Ridge dominates here with R²=0.84 — meaning 84% of stress change variance is explained. This is strong** *uitgelegd als in er is correlatie met de luistersessies?,*.
  - The main reason: post_stress_mean and baseline_deviation_entry are highly predictive. If you were already below your baseline stress, you
   tend to recover more; if you started high, you tend to drop more.

  Key Ridge coefficients for mood_delta:
  - playlist_energy (+1.19) — energy playlist associated with bigger mood improvements
  - mood_before_score (-0.93) — if you start in a good mood, the delta is smaller (ceiling effect — expected)
  - playlist_calm (+0.69) — calm also positive, just less so

  ---
  What SHAP adds

  SHAP explains individual predictions — for a specific session, which features pushed the prediction up or down. The warnings ("BORDERLINE",
   "LOW reliability") are honest about the fact that with N=40 or less per participant, SHAP patterns are directional at best, not
  statistically robust.

  ---
  Bottom line: The stress model is genuinely predictive (R²=0.84). The mood model shows a real signal (R²=0.25) but mood is inherently
  noisier. **The strongest finding is that baseline deviation and playlist type are the most meaningful predictors** — which directly supports
  the research hypothesis.

● Here's what the significance tests mean and what the results say:                                                                          
                                                                                                                                             
  ---                                                                                                                                        
  Three types of tests                                                                                                                       
                                                                                                                                             
  1. Immediate session effects (Wilcoxon signed-rank)                                                                                        
  Compares pre-session stress/HR against during- or post-session values, per session. The Wilcoxon is a non-parametric paired test — it asks:
   is the difference consistently positive or negative, or just random noise?                                                                

  2. Mood effect (one-sample Wilcoxon)
  Tests whether mood_delta ≠ 0 per playlist type. Did mood actually change, or did it just fluctuate around zero?

  3. Long-term trend (OLS regression)
  Fits a line over pre_study_stress_deviation across session numbers. Is the participant's baseline-relative stress going up or down over
  time as the study progresses?

  ---
  What the results say

  Only 4 tests came back significant (p < 0.05):

  ┌──────────────┬────────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Participant  │            Test            │                                      Finding                                      │
  ├──────────────┼────────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ peer         │ mood_delta_Calm            │ p=0.047, effect=0.57 — mood worsened after calm playlists                         │
  ├──────────────┼────────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ peer         │ trend_over_sessions stress │ p=0.004, effect=-0.93 — stress deviation is decreasing over time (getting better) │
  ├──────────────┼────────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ kokosnoot    │ trend_over_sessions stress │ p=0.016, effect=-0.49 — same trend, stress deviation decreasing                   │
  ├──────────────┼────────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ (all others) │ all immediate effects      │ Not significant                                                                   │
  └──────────────┴────────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  ---
  What this means in plain terms

  No immediate session effects were significant. For both peer and kokosnoot, stress and HR during/after sessions are not measurably
  different from before — at least not consistently enough to pass a statistical test. This could mean:
  - The effect is real but small relative to the noise
  - N per subgroup is too low (e.g., 8–13 sessions per playlist type for Wilcoxon)
  - Stress changes happen but vary too much between sessions

  The long-term trend is the clearest signal. **Both kokosnoot (p=0.016) and peer (p=0.004) show stress deviation decreasing over session**
  number. This means: relative to each person's normal circadian stress level, they're arriving at sessions less stressed as the study goes
  on. Whether that's caused by the music, by habituation to the study, or by something else entirely, can't be determined from this alone —
  but it's a consistent pattern in both participants.

  **The mood worsening after Calm playlists (peer, p=0.047) is counterintuitive**. The effect size is 0.57 (moderate), based on 12 sessions. One
  plausible explanation: a calm playlist when you weren't feeling particularly stressed may feel boring or subduing. Worth flagging but the N
   is small enough that this could also be noise.

  ---
  The key honest takeaway: **The study has a genuine long-term stress reduction signal, but the per-session effects are too noisy with current**
  N to reach significance. More sessions = more statistical power.
